Thailand's commanding market dominance—approximately 20 million annual arrivals—substantially exceeds Vietnam (15 million, 75% differential) and Cambodia (5 million, 300% differential). Most significantly, these hierarchical positions demonstrate remarkable stability throughout the decade, indicating entrenchment resulting from immutable first-mover advantages rather than transitory competitive fluctuation.

Vietnam's intermediate positioning reflects partial emergence from geopolitical isolation combined with expanding infrastructure development, increasing international air connectivity, and growing destination awareness among tourism markets. Cambodia's substantially constrained market share despite exceptional cultural significance (Angkor Wat's archaeological importance) reveals profound marketing deficiencies and infrastructure development gaps constraining competitive participation despite possessing authentic cultural attractions comparable to Thailand.

The remarkable market structure consistency across ten years indicates reversing established hierarchies would require extraordinary coordinated interventions transcending conventional competitive mechanisms. Strategic intervention requires comprehensive regional approaches encompassing infrastructure development acceleration, joint ASEAN marketing initiatives, visitor distribution mechanisms alleviating Thailand's overtourism pressures, and deliberate market segmentation emphasizing Cambodia and Vietnam's distinctive competitive advantages.
